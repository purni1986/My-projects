RSSMD (Root mean square of successive differences)

MSSD (Root Mean Square of Successive Differences) is a widely used and highly regarded time-domain measure of Heart Rate Variability (HRV). It specifically reflects the short-term, beat-to-beat variability in heart rate and is considered a strong indicator of parasympathetic nervous system (PNS) or vagal tone activity.
Here's what we can infer from RMSSD values:
Vagal Tone and Parasympathetic Activity:
Higher RMSSD values indicate higher vagal tone and more robust parasympathetic nervous system activity. This suggests that the body is in a more relaxed state, capable of efficient "rest and digest" functions and recovery.
Lower RMSSD values suggest reduced vagal tone and diminished parasympathetic influence. This often points towards a state of stress, fatigue, or less efficient recovery mechanisms.
Stress and Recovery:
During periods of stress (physical or psychological), RMSSD typically decreases, as the sympathetic nervous system (SNS) dominates.
During periods of relaxation, sleep, or effective recovery, RMSSD tends to increase, reflecting the re-engagement of the PNS.
Cardiac Health and Autonomic Control:
A healthy and adaptable heart typically exhibits higher RMSSD values, indicating a well-regulated autonomic nervous system capable of responding flexibly to various demands.
Significantly low RMSSD values can be associated with increased risk of cardiovascular events, as they suggest impaired autonomic regulation and reduced cardiac adaptability.
It's a valuable metric in assessing the overall health and resilience of the cardiovascular system.

What is generally considered a "high" RMSSD?
Higher values are generally indicative of a healthy, well-regulated autonomic nervous system with robust parasympathetic (rest and digest) activity. This suggests good recovery capacity, resilience to stress, and a state of relaxation.
Typical ranges for healthy adults at rest can vary significantly, often extending from below 20 to over 70 milliseconds (ms), with young athletes potentially exhibiting values exceeding 200ms.
Younger individuals and highly fit individuals tend to have higher RMSSD values. For example, normative data suggests median RMSSD values around 60ms for individuals in their late teens/early twenties, decreasing with age. 
What is generally considered a "low" RMSSD?
Lower values typically suggest reduced parasympathetic activity and potentially heightened sympathetic (fight or flight) activity, often associated with stress, fatigue, illness, or poor recovery.
Consistently low RMSSD values, especially when compared to an individual's baseline or age-matched norms, can be a cause for concern and may be associated with increased risk of cardiovascular issues or other health problems.

The interbeat interval is the time between successive heartbeats, so a shorter interval signifies a faster heart rate. 
Interpreting what this actually means clinically depends on several factors, as a lower-than-average heart rate can be a sign of both good health and an underlying medical condition.

Potential interpretations
1. Excellent cardiovascular fitness
A slower-than-average resting heart rate (bradycardia) is common and normal in highly conditioned athletes and very fit individuals. Their hearts are so strong that they can pump a larger volume of blood with each beat, meaning fewer beats are needed to circulate the same amount of oxygen. 
2. Normal for the individual
A lower average IBI (faster heart rate) may be perfectly normal for that specific patient. Many factors influence heart rate, including genetics, age, and normal day-to-day variations. Looking at one patient's IBI value in isolation without considering their medical history is not advised. 
3. Sign of a medical condition
In non-athletic individuals, a consistently lower-than-average IBI can be a sign of underlying issues. A fast heart rate (tachycardia) can be caused by a variety of conditions that include:

Hypothyroidism: An underactive thyroid gland can affect the heart's electrical system and slow its rhythm.
Heart damage: Prior heart attacks or other heart diseases can cause damage to the heart tissue that affects the heart's electrical signaling.
Chemical imbalances: An imbalance of electrolytes in the blood can disrupt the heart's electrical signals.
Certain medications: Some medications, such as beta-blockers, can lower heart rate.

High interbeat interval (heart function)
The interbeat interval (IBI) is the time between successive heartbeats, measured in milliseconds. A high IBI means the heart is beating slowly. 
Relationship to heart rate: IBI is inversely related to heart rate. A longer IBI means a slower heart rate. For example, an IBI of 1000 milliseconds equates to a heart rate of 60 beats per minute.
Significance:
An increase in IBI (slowing of the heart rate) can be a normal, temporary response to relaxation or focused attention.
While normal heart function involves natural, beat-to-beat variations in the IBI (known as Heart Rate Variability or HRV), an IBI that is consistently high can indicate an abnormally slow heart rate (bradycardia).
An extremely high IBI or a low HRV can be a sign of underlying cardiac conditions or other illnesses, and warrants further investigation.
